Interpretation of the monitoring data and related Philippine events.

Supply movement narrative

The expanded monitoring records now show 19,738.80 MT of inbound volume and 12,035.29 MT of outbound volume from January 01, 2024 to June 01, 2026. The overall outbound-to-inbound movement is 61.0%, leaving a positive recorded net balance of 7,703.51 MT. Year coverage summary: 2024: 9,707.52 MT inbound / 5,628.76 MT outbound; 2025: 6,560.44 MT inbound / 4,058.42 MT outbound; 2026: 3,470.84 MT inbound / 2,348.12 MT outbound.

The 2024 data starts under dry-season and El Niño-related pressure, where farm losses and local state-of-calamity declarations were reported in several parts of the Philippines. This makes the January-May 2024 supply movement important for identifying which vegetables still entered Bulua despite water stress, reduced farm productivity, and transport cost pressure.

The highest monitored inbound month is Jan 2024 (1,587.96 MT), while the lowest monitored month is Jan 2025 (115.80 MT). For the 2025 annual view, January-May comes from the added monthly files and June-December comes only from the ConsolidatedYear sheet of the latest updated 2025 workbook.

Philippine events linked to supply conditions

These events provide context for interpreting supply movement, not direct proof of causation for every commodity.

Early 2024: El Niño and dry spell

Dry conditions affected agricultural production in several areas of the Philippines. For Bulua monitoring, this is a supply-risk period for water-sensitive and highland vegetables.

July 2024: Habagat, Butchoy, and Carina

Monsoon rains and tropical cyclone effects caused flooding, road disruption, and logistics delays. These conditions can reduce same-week arrivals and later create rebound deliveries once transport routes normalize.

Oct-Nov 2024: Successive cyclones

Kristine, Leon, Nika, Ofel, and Pepito contributed to a storm-heavy period. For supply interpretation, late-2024 arrivals should be checked against crop damage, harvesting delays, and inter-island movement constraints.

July 2025: Habagat + Crising, Dante, Emong

Heavy rains, flooding, damaged crops, and transport disruption affected agricultural operations. High-value crops and perishable vegetables were vulnerable to reduced supply arrivals and price pressure.

Late 2025: Replanting and supply recovery

Recovery and replanting helped stabilize the supply narrative after weather shocks, especially for highland vegetables that rely on coordinated harvesting and transport.

2026: Climate transition risk

PAGASA ended La Niña in March 2026 and later advised preparedness for possible El Niño by mid-2026. This is relevant for production planning and inventory watch in the second half of 2026.

Data quality notes

For 2025, January-May monitoring files were added from the monthly ZIP files, and June-December values were taken only from the ConsolidatedYear sheet of the latest updated 2025 workbook. Dates in the 2025 workbook that appeared outside 2025 were normalized to 2025 while preserving month/day. In the latest ConsolidatedYear extraction, August 2025 is corrected at 549.22 MT inbound and 373.35 MT outbound, producing a positive net balance of 175.88 MT. No August row in the updated source has outflow greater than inflow. For 2026, blank dates were carried forward from the previous valid record. Commodity spelling variants such as Umboc/Umbok, Brocolli/Broccoli, and Sweetcorn/Sweet Corn were consolidated for filtering.
